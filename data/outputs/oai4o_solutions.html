Q12. (i) Define simple harmonic motion. [1] (ii) Derive an expression for the time period of oscillation for a mass m attached to a vertical spring of force constant k. (iii) What will be the time period of this system if it is taken inside the satellite?

Answer

Q12. Simple Harmonic Motion and Time Period of a Mass-Spring System

(i) Define simple harmonic motion. [1]

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of periodic motion where the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement from the equilibrium position and acts in the direction opposite to that displacement. Mathematically, it can be described by the equation: [ F = -kx ] where ( F ) is the restoring force, ( k ) is the force constant (or spring constant), and ( x ) is the displacement from the equilibrium position.

(ii) Derive an expression for the time period of oscillation for a mass ( m ) attached to a vertical spring of force constant ( k ).

To derive the time period ( T ) of a mass-spring system, we start with Hooke's Law and Newton's second law of motion.

  1. Hooke's Law: The restoring force ( F ) is given by: [ F = -kx ]

  2. Newton's Second Law: The force is also equal to the mass ( m ) times the acceleration ( a ): [ F = ma ]

  3. Combining the two equations: [ ma = -kx ] Since acceleration ( a ) is the second derivative of displacement ( x ) with respect to time ( t ): [ m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -kx ]

  4. Rearranging the equation: [ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \frac{k}{m}x = 0 ]

This is a second-order differential equation of the form: [ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \omega^2 x = 0 ] where ( \omega ) (angular frequency) is: [ \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} ]

  1. Time Period ( T ): The time period ( T ) is related to the angular frequency ( \omega ) by: [ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} ]

Substituting ( \omega ): [ T = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}} ] [ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} ]

Thus, the time period of oscillation for a mass ( m ) attached to a vertical spring of force constant ( k ) is: [ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} ]

(iii) What will be the time period of this system if it is taken inside the satellite?

When the system is taken inside a satellite, it is in a state of free fall, experiencing microgravity. In this condition, the gravitational force does not affect the oscillation of the mass-spring system. The time period of the mass-spring system depends only on the mass ( m ) and the spring constant ( k ), and not on the gravitational acceleration ( g ).

Therefore, the time period ( T ) inside the satellite remains the same as derived in part (ii): [ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} ]

In conclusion, the time period of the mass-spring system inside the satellite is unchanged and is given by: [ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} ]

Q13. (a) State Bernoulli's Principle.

Bernoulli's Principle states that for an incompressible, non-viscous fluid flowing in a streamline, the sum of the pressure energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy per unit volume remains constant along any streamline. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

[ P + \frac{1}{2} \rho v^2 + \rho gh = \text{constant} ]

where:

  • ( P ) is the pressure within the fluid,
  • ( \rho ) is the fluid density,
  • ( v ) is the fluid velocity,
  • ( g ) is the acceleration due to gravity,
  • ( h ) is the height above a reference point.

(i) The speed at B

To find the speed at B, we use the principle of continuity, which states that the product of the cross-sectional area and the velocity of the fluid is constant along the streamline. Mathematically:

[ A_A v_A = A_B v_B ]

  • ( A_B = 20 , \text{mm}^2 = 20 \times 10^{-6} , \text{m}^2 )
  • ( v_A = 10 , \text{cm/s} = 0.1 , \text{m/s} )

We need to find ( v_B ):

[ v_B = \frac{A_A v_A}{A_B} ]

(ii) The difference in pressure at A and B

[ P_A + \frac{1}{2} \rho v_A^2 = P_B + \frac{1}{2} \rho v_B^2 ]

We need to find the difference in pressure ( \Delta P = P_A - P_B ):

[ \Delta P = \frac{1}{2} \rho (v_B^2 - v_A^2) ]

Q14. (a) Draw a PV diagram of a petrol engine and explain its working based on its PV diagram.

PV Diagram of a Petrol Engine

A petrol engine operates on the Otto cycle, which consists of two adiabatic processes and two isochoric processes. The PV diagram for an Otto cycle is shown below:

Explanation of the Working Based on the PV Diagram

  1. Process 1-2 (Adiabatic Compression):

    • The piston compresses the air-fuel mixture adiabatically (no heat exchange with surroundings).
    • Volume decreases, pressure and temperature increase.

  2. Process 2-3 (Isochoric Heat Addition):

    • The spark plug ignites the compressed air-fuel mixture.
    • Volume remains constant, pressure and temperature increase rapidly.

  3. Process 3-4 (Adiabatic Expansion):

    • The high-pressure gases expand adiabatically, pushing the piston down.
    • Volume increases, pressure and temperature decrease.

  4. Process 4-1 (Isochoric Heat Rejection):

    • The exhaust valve opens, and the burnt gases are expelled.
    • Volume remains constant, pressure and temperature drop.

Q14. (b) Compare the efficiency of petrol engine with that of diesel engine based on their compression ratios.

Efficiency Comparison Based on Compression Ratios

  • Petrol Engine (Otto Cycle):

    • Efficiency ((\eta_{\text{Otto}})) is given by: [ \eta_{\text{Otto}} = 1 - \left(\frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}\right) ] where (r) is the compression ratio and (\gamma) is the specific heat ratio (typically 1.4 for air).

  • Diesel Engine (Diesel Cycle):

    • Efficiency ((\eta_{\text{Diesel}})) is given by: [ \eta_{\text{Diesel}} = 1 - \left(\frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}\right) \left(\frac{\rho^{\gamma} - 1}{\gamma(\rho - 1)}\right) ] where (r) is the compression ratio, (\gamma) is the specific heat ratio, and (\rho) is the cut-off ratio (the ratio of the volume after combustion to the volume before combustion).

Comparison:

  • Compression Ratio:

    • Petrol engines typically have lower compression ratios (8:1 to 12:1) compared to diesel engines (14:1 to 22:1).
    • Higher compression ratios in diesel engines lead to higher thermal efficiency.

  • Efficiency:

    • Due to higher compression ratios, diesel engines are generally more efficient than petrol engines.
    • The efficiency of a diesel engine increases with an increase in compression ratio more significantly than that of a petrol engine.

In summary, diesel engines are more efficient than petrol engines primarily due to their higher compression ratios, which result in better fuel efficiency and higher thermal efficiency.

Q16.(a) State Lenz law in electromagnetism. Justify this law is in favor of the principle of conservation of energy.(b) A magnet is quickly moved in the direction indicated by an arrow between two coils C1 and C2 as shown in the figure. What will be the direction of induced current in each coil as indicated by the movement of magnet? Explain.

Answer

Q16. (a)

Lenz's Law:

Lenz's Law states that the direction of the induced electromotive force (emf) and hence the induced current in a closed loop is such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

[ \mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} ]

where:

  • (\mathcal{E}) is the induced emf,
  • (\Phi_B) is the magnetic flux.

Justification with Conservation of Energy:

Lenz's Law is consistent with the principle of conservation of energy. When a change in magnetic flux induces a current, the direction of the induced current is such that it creates a magnetic field opposing the change in the original magnetic flux. This opposition requires work to be done against the induced magnetic field, ensuring that energy is conserved. If the induced current did not oppose the change, it would lead to a perpetual increase in energy, violating the conservation of energy principle.

Q16. (b)

Direction of Induced Current:

Given the figure, let's analyze the direction of the induced current in each coil (C1 and C2) as the magnet is moved quickly in the direction indicated by the arrow.

  1. Coil C1:

    • As the north pole of the magnet approaches coil C1, the magnetic flux through C1 increases.
    • According to Lenz's Law, the induced current in C1 will flow in a direction that opposes this increase in magnetic flux.
    • To oppose the approaching north pole, coil C1 will generate its own north pole on the side facing the magnet.
    • Using the right-hand rule, the current in coil C1 will flow in a counterclockwise direction when viewed from the magnet's side.

  2. Coil C2:

    • As the north pole of the magnet moves away from coil C2, the magnetic flux through C2 decreases.
    • According to Lenz's Law, the induced current in C2 will flow in a direction that opposes this decrease in magnetic flux.
    • To oppose the receding north pole, coil C2 will generate its own south pole on the side facing the magnet.
    • Using the right-hand rule, the current in coil C2 will flow in a clockwise direction when viewed from the magnet's side.

Explanation:

  • For coil C1, the induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes the increase in magnetic flux due to the approaching north pole of the magnet.
  • For coil C2, the induced current creates a magnetic field that opposes the decrease in magnetic flux due to the receding north pole of the magnet.

Thus, the directions of the induced currents are:

  • Coil C1: Counterclockwise
  • Coil C2: Clockwise

Q17. (a) Principle of the Potentiometer and Infinite Resistance Voltmeter

Q17. (a) Principle of the Potentiometer

The principle of the potentiometer is based on the fact that the potential drop across a uniform wire is directly proportional to its length, provided the wire has a uniform cross-sectional area and the current through it is constant. Mathematically, this can be expressed as:

[ V \propto l ]

where ( V ) is the potential difference and ( l ) is the length of the wire.

Q17. (a) Potentiometer as a Voltmeter of Infinite Resistance

A potentiometer is considered a voltmeter of infinite resistance because it does not draw any current from the circuit being measured. In a potentiometer, the potential difference is measured by balancing it against a known potential difference without allowing any current to flow through the galvanometer. This ensures that the measurement does not affect the circuit, which is equivalent to having an infinite resistance.

Q17. (b) Meter Bridge Experiment

Q17. (b) (i) New Position of Balance Point

Given that the initial balance point is at ( l = 20 ) cm, and the values of ( R ) and ( X ) are doubled and then interchanged, we need to determine the new balance point.

Initially, the balance condition is:

[ \frac{R}{X} = \frac{l}{100 - l} ]

Given ( l = 20 ) cm, we have:

[ \frac{R}{X} = \frac{20}{80} = \frac{1}{4} ]

When ( R ) and ( X ) are doubled and interchanged, the new resistances are ( 2X ) and ( 2R ). The new balance condition becomes:

[ \frac{2X}{2R} = \frac{l'}{100 - l'} ]

Since ( \frac{2X}{2R} = \frac{X}{R} = 4 ), we have:

[ 4 = \frac{l'}{100 - l'} ]

Solving for ( l' ):

[ 4(100 - l') = l' ] [ 400 - 4l' = l' ] [ 400 = 5l' ] [ l' = 80 \text{ cm} ]

Thus, the new position of the balance point is ( l' = 80 ) cm.

Q17. (b) (ii) Effect of Interchanging Galvanometer and Battery

If the galvanometer and battery are interchanged at the balance position, the balance point will not be affected. This is because the potentiometer works on the principle of null deflection, where the potential difference is balanced, and no current flows through the galvanometer. The position of the balance point depends only on the potential difference and the length of the wire, not on the position of the galvanometer or battery. Therefore, the balance point remains the same.

Free Body Diagram Analysis

  2. Buoyant Force (( F_b )): [ F_b = V \rho_{air} g ] where ( V ) is the volume of the oil drop and ( \rho_{air} ) is the density of air.

  3. Viscous Drag Force (( F_d )): [ F_d = 6 \pi \eta r v ] where ( \eta ) is the viscosity of air, ( r ) is the radius of the oil drop, and ( v ) is the terminal velocity.

At terminal velocity, the forces are balanced: [ F_g - F_b = F_d ]

Substituting the expressions for the forces: [ mg - V \rho_{air} g = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

The mass ( m ) of the oil drop can be expressed as: [ m = V \rho_{oil} ] where ( \rho_{oil} ) is the density of the oil.

The volume ( V ) of the spherical oil drop is: [ V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 ]

Substituting ( m ) and ( V ) into the force balance equation: [ \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \rho_{oil} \right) g - \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \rho_{air} \right) g = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

Simplifying: [ \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 (\rho_{oil} - \rho_{air}) g = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

Solving for ( v ): [ v = \frac{2 r^2 (\rho_{oil} - \rho_{air}) g}{9 \eta} ]

  2. Gravitational Force (( F_g )): [ F_g = mg ]

  3. Buoyant Force (( F_b )): [ F_b = V \rho_{air} g ]

  4. Viscous Drag Force (( F_d )): [ F_d = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

At terminal velocity, the forces are balanced: [ qE - (mg - V \rho_{air} g) = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

Substituting ( m ) and ( V ): [ qE - \left( \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \rho_{oil} g - \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \rho_{air} g \right) = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

Simplifying: [ qE - \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 (\rho_{oil} - \rho_{air}) g = 6 \pi \eta r v ]

To find the transition that results in the emission of a photon of wavelength 275 nm, we need to calculate the energy of the photon using the formula:

[ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} ]

  • ( h = 6.64 \times 10^{-34} , \text{Js} )

Q20. (c) Find the expression for the wavelength of radiation emitted from a hydrogen atom when an electron jumps from higher energy level ( n_2 ) to the lower energy level ( n_1 ).

[ E_n = - \frac{13.6 , \text{eV}}{n^2} ]

[ \Delta E = E_{n_2} - E_{n_1} = - \frac{13.6 , \text{eV}}{n_2^2} + \frac{13.6 , \text{eV}}{n_1^2} ] [ \Delta E = 13.6 , \text{eV} \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right) ]

The wavelength of the emitted radiation is given by:

[ \lambda = \frac{hc}{\Delta E} ]

Substituting the value of ( \Delta E ):

[ \lambda = \frac{hc}{13.6 , \text{eV} \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right)} ]

[ \frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right) ]

[ n_1 = 2, , n_2 = 4 ]

Q21. In what way does the intensity of sound heard by an observer change if the distance with the source changes by four times? b) A train is traveling at 30m/s in still air. The frequency of the note emitted by the train whistle is 262Hz. What frequency is heard by a passenger on the other train moving in the opposite direction to the first at 18m/s (i) when approaches the first and (ii) when receding from the first?( velocity of sound= 340 m/s) c) In a sinusoidal sound wave of moderate loudness, the maximum pressure variations are of the order of 3.0x10-2 Pa above and below the atmospheric pressure. Find the corresponding maximum displacement if the frequency is 1000Hz.in air at normal atmospheric pressure and density. The speed of sound is 344m/s and the bulk modulus of the medium is 1.42x105 Pa.

Answer

Q21.

(a) Change in Intensity with Distance

The intensity of sound ( I ) is inversely proportional to the square of the distance ( r ) from the source. This relationship is given by the inverse square law:

[ I \propto \frac{1}{r^2} ]

If the distance from the source changes by four times, the new distance ( r' ) is:

[ r' = 4r ]

The new intensity ( I' ) can be expressed as:

[ I' \propto \frac{1}{(4r)^2} = \frac{1}{16r^2} ]

Thus, the intensity of the sound heard by the observer will be:

[ I' = \frac{I}{16} ]

So, the intensity of the sound decreases by a factor of 16 when the distance is increased by four times.

(b) Frequency Heard by a Passenger on Another Train

The Doppler effect formula for sound when both the source and the observer are moving is:

[ f' = f \left( \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s} \right) ]

where:

  • ( f' ) is the observed frequency,
  • ( f ) is the emitted frequency (262 Hz),
  • ( v ) is the speed of sound in air (340 m/s),
  • ( v_o ) is the speed of the observer (18 m/s),
  • ( v_s ) is the speed of the source (30 m/s).

(i) When the observer is approaching the source:

[ f' = 262 \left( \frac{340 + 18}{340 - 30} \right) ]

[ f' = 262 \left( \frac{358}{310} \right) ]

[ f' \approx 262 \times 1.1548 ]

[ f' \approx 302.56 \text{ Hz} ]

(ii) When the observer is receding from the source:

[ f' = 262 \left( \frac{340 - 18}{340 + 30} \right) ]

[ f' = 262 \left( \frac{322}{370} \right) ]

[ f' \approx 262 \times 0.8703 ]

[ f' \approx 228.82 \text{ Hz} ]

(c) Maximum Displacement in a Sinusoidal Sound Wave

The relationship between the maximum pressure variation ( \Delta P_{\text{max}} ) and the maximum displacement ( s_{\text{max}} ) in a sound wave is given by:

[ \Delta P_{\text{max}} = B k s_{\text{max}} ]

where:

  • ( B ) is the bulk modulus of the medium (1.42 × 10(^5) Pa),
  • ( k ) is the wave number,
  • ( s_{\text{max}} ) is the maximum displacement.

The wave number ( k ) is related to the wavelength ( \lambda ) by:

[ k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} ]

The wavelength ( \lambda ) can be found using the speed of sound ( v ) and the frequency ( f ):

[ \lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{344 \text{ m/s}}{1000 \text{ Hz}} = 0.344 \text{ m} ]

Thus,

[ k = \frac{2\pi}{0.344} \approx 18.26 \text{ m}^{-1} ]

Now, substituting the values into the pressure variation equation:

[ 3.0 \times 10^{-2} \text{ Pa} = 1.42 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa} \times 18.26 \text{ m}^{-1} \times s_{\text{max}} ]

Solving for ( s_{\text{max}} ):

[ s_{\text{max}} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^{-2}}{1.42 \times 10^5 \times 18.26} ]

[ s_{\text{max}} \approx \frac{3.0 \times 10^{-2}}{2.591 \times 10^6} ]

[ s_{\text{max}} \approx 1.16 \times 10^{-8} \text{ m} ]

So, the maximum displacement is approximately ( 1.16 \times 10^{-8} \text{ m} ).

Answer

Q22. (a) What is choke coil?

A choke coil is an inductor used to block higher-frequency alternating current (AC) in an electrical circuit while allowing lower-frequency or direct current (DC) to pass. It typically consists of a coil of wire wound around a magnetic core. The inductance of the coil creates a reactance that impedes the flow of AC, especially at higher frequencies.

Q22. (b) Why is it preferred over a resistor in AC circuit?

A choke coil is preferred over a resistor in an AC circuit because it can limit the current without dissipating much power as heat. In a resistor, the power dissipation is given by ( P = I^2 R ), where ( I ) is the current and ( R ) is the resistance. This results in energy loss in the form of heat. In contrast, a choke coil primarily stores energy in its magnetic field and releases it back into the circuit, resulting in minimal energy loss. This makes the choke coil more efficient for controlling AC current.

Q22. (c) In figures (a), (b), and (c) in {placeholder}, three AC circuits with equal currents have been shown.

Q22. (c)(i) If the frequency of e.m.f. is increased, then what will be the effect on the currents flowing in them? Explain.

To answer this, we need to consider the impedance of each circuit component:

  • Inductor (L): The impedance ( Z_L = j\omega L ), where ( \omega = 2\pi f ) is the angular frequency. As the frequency ( f ) increases, the inductive reactance ( \omega L ) increases, thus reducing the current.
  • Capacitor (C): The impedance ( Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} ). As the frequency ( f ) increases, the capacitive reactance ( \frac{1}{\omega C} ) decreases, thus increasing the current.
  • Resistor (R): The impedance ( Z_R = R ) is independent of frequency, so the current remains unchanged with frequency.

Therefore:

  • In the circuit with an inductor (figure (a)), the current will decrease as the frequency increases.
  • In the circuit with a capacitor (figure (b)), the current will increase as the frequency increases.
  • In the circuit with a resistor (figure (c)), the current will remain unchanged as the frequency increases.

When the AC e.m.f. is replaced by its equivalent DC e.m.f.:

  • Capacitor (figure (b)): For DC, the frequency ( f = 0 ), so the capacitive reactance ( \frac{1}{\omega C} ) becomes infinite. The capacitor behaves like an open circuit, offering infinite opposition to the DC current.

In summary:

  • The capacitor will provide infinite opposition to the DC current.
